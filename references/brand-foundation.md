# Brand Foundation

Use this reference when the brand context is incomplete or a reusable design foundation is required.

## Foundation canvas

Capture only information that changes design decisions.

### 1. Brand reality

- What does the organization make, provide, or enable?
- In what real situations does the value become visible?
- What can the brand credibly prove?
- What must never be implied without evidence?

### 2. Audience

- Who is the primary decision-maker or user?
- What are they trying to accomplish?
- What do they already know?
- What uncertainty prevents trust or action?
- Which secondary audiences require different routes or detail?

### 3. Desired memory

Complete this sentence with a specific idea:

> After visiting, the primary audience should remember that this brand ______.

Reject answers made only of adjectives. Prefer a capability, relationship, behavior, or point of view.

### 4. Brand world

List concrete sources:

- environments;
- materials and objects;
- people and behaviors;
- product or service mechanics;
- history and culture;
- language used by customers;
- authentic images, documents, data, demonstrations, or stories.

Use these sources to derive the design. Do not use mood-board resemblance as the only rationale.

### 5. Value evidence

For each important claim, identify the best proof:

| Claim | Evidence | Best expression | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| What the brand says | Product behavior, result, data, case, process, certification, testimony | Demonstration, image, diagram, copy, interaction | Confirmed, provisional, missing |

Do not replace missing evidence with decorative statistics or stronger adjectives.

## Translate adjectives into behavior

Ask what the adjective changes and how failure would be recognized.

| Abstract input | Resolve through questions such as |
|---|---|
| Premium | What creates value: material, service, rarity, precision, heritage, performance, or restraint? |
| Modern | What is being improved: behavior, technology, organization, expression, or accessibility? |
| Bold | Should the brand challenge a convention, dominate attention, speak directly, or use strong contrast? |
| Trustworthy | What proof, transparency, consistency, or risk reduction earns trust? |
| Human | Which people, language, imperfections, relationships, or acts of care make this true? |
| Innovative | What genuinely works differently, and how can the audience observe it? |

Do not translate adjectives directly into colors or effects.

## Define visual behavior

Specify relationships, not isolated tokens, unless tokens already exist.

### Structure and space

- hierarchy, alignment, density, asymmetry, modularity, and use of negative space;
- when structure should feel stable, flexible, editorial, immersive, or task-oriented.

### Typography

- voice, scale relationships, reading density, emphasis, and functional roles;
- how type supports the offering rather than becoming unrelated spectacle.

### Color and light

- source in the brand world;
- roles for background, text, evidence, state, and emphasis;
- light/dark behavior, contrast, and user preference;
- conditions under which the palette should change.

### Imagery and material

- subject, context, scale, perspective, lighting, realism, and evidence value;
- what to avoid and why;
- how products, services, people, and environments relate.

### Motion and interaction

- what change, function, sequence, or spatial relationship motion should reveal;
- pace and restraint;
- reduced-motion behavior;
- which interactions are essential to understanding.

### Content

- proof before or alongside claims;
- audience vocabulary;
- sequence from orientation to confidence;
- where technical depth, comparison, or documentation belongs.

## Separate scopes

- Put cross-project, repeatedly validated decisions in the brand foundation.
- Put campaign, page, audience, or temporary choices in a project decision record.
- Keep hypotheses labeled until validated.
- Remove rules that no longer have a reason, boundary, or observable effect.
