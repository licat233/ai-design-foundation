# Exploration and Decisions

Use this reference when direction is uncertain, several stakeholders disagree, or an agent is asked to produce multiple concepts.

## Frame the decision

Write the uncertainty as a decision question.

Weak:

> Should the hero use video?

Stronger:

> What opening evidence will help first-time buyers understand the offering before they evaluate details?

The stronger question leaves the medium open and makes alternatives comparable.

## Decide whether exploration is needed

Explore when different answers could materially change:

- brand perception;
- audience understanding;
- product or service positioning;
- narrative priority;
- opening experience;
- content or evidence strategy;
- imagery, motion, or interaction role.

Do not create concept rounds for routine spacing, established components, or low-risk reversible choices.

## Create meaningful alternatives

Each direction must differ in at least one strategic dimension:

- what the audience understands first;
- what evidence carries the story;
- what role the brand plays;
- how the experience earns trust;
- what organizing metaphor or behavior structures the page.

Changing palette, font, corner radius, or image position alone does not create a direction.

## Direction card

For every candidate, record:

### Name

Use a descriptive name, not a style label.

### Hypothesis

State why this direction may solve the decision question.

### Audience effect

State what the audience should notice, understand, feel, or do.

### Evidence

State which product behavior, case, content, research, image, or prototype supports it.

### Execution implications

Describe likely effects on content, hierarchy, imagery, motion, interaction, and implementation.

### Risks

State failure modes, missing assets, false assumptions, and what would disconfirm the direction.

## Compare

Compare all candidates against the same criteria:

- objective fit;
- audience clarity;
- brand specificity;
- evidence strength;
- differentiation without novelty for its own sake;
- feasibility and content readiness;
- accessibility and task performance;
- long-term usefulness when relevant.

Do not average directions into a compromise unless the combined logic remains coherent.

## Stop exploration

Decide when:

- the decision question is clear;
- meaningful alternatives were considered;
- one direction has stronger reasons or evidence;
- consequences and risks are understood;
- remaining uncertainty is visible and acceptable;
- execution can proceed without hiding a consequential guess.

Do not require certainty that can only come from implementation or real use. Define the next validation instead.

## Decision record

```markdown
# Design Decision: <title>

## Question
What needed to be decided?

## Context
Why does it matter for the brand, audience, or task?

## Alternatives
Which meaningfully different answers were considered?

## Evidence
What observations, assets, prototypes, research, or constraints informed the choice?

## Decision
What direction was selected?

## Rationale
Why is it the strongest current answer?

## Implications
What changes in content, layout, imagery, motion, interaction, or implementation?

## Scope
Brand-wide, project-specific, page-specific, or experiment only?

## Confidence
Confirmed, provisional, or exploratory?

## Open questions and validation
What remains unknown, and how will it be tested?
```
