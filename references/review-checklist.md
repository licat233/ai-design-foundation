# Website Design Review

Review in order. Do not polish a downstream layer while an upstream decision is wrong.

Use **pass**, **needs evidence**, or **revise** for each relevant question.

## 1. Objective

- Is the primary audience and task clear?
- Does the page solve the problem it was created to solve?
- Is the intended next action appropriate and earned?
- Are important assumptions visible?

## 2. Brand specificity

- Would the experience remain recognizable without the logo?
- Does the direction come from the brand's real world, behavior, evidence, or point of view?
- Are abstract attributes expressed through specific relationships rather than clichés?
- Could the same design belong to an unrelated competitor with only content swaps?
- Are familiar patterns used for clarity rather than by default?

## 3. Offering and evidence

- Is the product, service, mission, or value the source of the story?
- Are important claims demonstrated or supported?
- Are data, cases, testimonials, certifications, and comparisons contextualized?
- Does imagery reveal real use, behavior, scale, material, people, or results?
- Does decoration compete with the evidence?

## 4. Experience and content

- Does the opening accomplish one clear objective?
- Does each major moment answer the next audience question or reduce uncertainty?
- Is information ordered by audience understanding rather than internal organization?
- Are technical detail and reassurance available when needed?
- Do density, pace, emphasis, and quiet moments support meaning?
- Is the CTA a natural continuation rather than a repeated demand?

## 5. Visual language

- Is hierarchy clear before effects are considered?
- Do layout, space, typography, color, imagery, iconography, and motion express one coherent direction?
- Does color come from the brand context and perform defined roles?
- Does typography support voice and reading rather than unrelated spectacle?
- Does motion reveal function, sequence, relationship, or feedback?
- Are repetitions meaningful, and are differences meaningful?

## 6. Usability and accessibility

- Can users orient, navigate, compare, and act without guessing?
- Are semantic structure, labels, focus, keyboard behavior, and interactive states clear?
- Is text readable across sizes and devices?
- Do text, controls, and meaningful graphics have sufficient contrast?
- Is information conveyed by more than color alone?
- Are reduced motion, zoom, reflow, and light/dark preferences handled when relevant?
- Does media include appropriate text alternatives, captions, or transcripts?

## 7. Implementation

- Does the implementation preserve the approved intent across breakpoints?
- Does real content fit without breaking hierarchy?
- Are loading, performance, and interaction delays compatible with the experience?
- Are components consistent without flattening meaningful differences?
- Are visual details robust rather than screenshot-only approximations?
- Have key flows been tested in the browser on representative devices?

## Report findings

For every non-pass item, state:

1. **Finding** — what is observable;
2. **Impact** — which audience, objective, or principle is affected;
3. **Cause** — direction, content, asset, hierarchy, accessibility, or implementation;
4. **Revision** — the smallest change that addresses the cause;
5. **Validation** — how to know the revision worked.

Prioritize findings:

- **Critical**: blocks understanding, access, trust, or task completion;
- **Major**: weakens direction, brand specificity, evidence, or hierarchy;
- **Minor**: improves consistency or polish without changing the core outcome.

Do not use “I like” or “I dislike” as evidence.
